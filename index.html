The premise

An empire built on a golden illusion, and the boy who walks out of it.

Charles Dunn, born into poverty in Macon, Georgia, becomes entangled with the powerful Grant family — first as a factory boy paying off a break-in, eventually as Thomas Grant's son-in-law and the man left holding the wreckage of his empire. The novel asks what a person owes to the family that raised them, the family that used them, and the name they choose to give their own child.

"From afar, what seemed like a golden treasure waiting to be explored, was a façade, an illusion. Similar to our dreams, our desires. Our way of life and our future."

Narrated, per the author's intention, by Dr. Edgar Kingston — town psychiatrist, father of a son lost to the same war that hollowed out Macon's men, and the only person who treats both families.

Top priority — revised

Resolve whether Thomas set the library fire that killed Alice.

A closer pass through Part Three turned up a fully-written Charles/Sophia courtship (the Marietta drive, the Asheville flat tire, the pecan orchard, the magnolia-field proposal) — so the marriage is better earned than first thought. The real open wound is Alice's death: Thomas is inside the building, pulls two clerks out, and swears he didn't start it, but the manuscript never settles the question, and Charles never gets a clean answer either. That ambiguity carries enormous weight and currently gets resolved only by exhaustion, not by craft.
